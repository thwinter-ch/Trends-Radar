## Essence of the Report

EY's Q4 2025 CEO Outlook Survey (1,200 global CEOs across 21 countries) identifies 2026 as a "turning point"—a decisive shift from piloting technologies to scaling them enterprise-wide. The report's central tension: CEO confidence is declining (index dropped from 83.0 to 78.5 quarter-over-quarter), yet 97% have begun or plan significant transformation initiatives this year.

This is not contradiction—it's pragmatic positioning. CEOs are uncertain about external conditions (global economy, geopolitics) but bullish about their own operations: 90% expect revenue growth and productivity gains to support profitability in 2026. They're doubling down on control variables (AI, M&A, operational optimization) while acknowledging limited control over macro headwinds.

The AI narrative shows a critical shift: **58% expect AI to be a major growth engine within two years**, while 32% believe it will fundamentally reshape operations. But only 20% report AI has significantly exceeded expectations—a signal that enthusiasm outpaces current performance.

Perhaps most revealing: the proportion of CEOs believing AI will reduce headcount dropped from 46% (January 2025) to 24% (December 2025). This isn't because AI got less capable—it's because leaders realized workforce transformation is more complex than simple displacement. **69% now believe AI will maintain or expand employment**, suggesting a reframe from "cost reduction through automation" to "productivity enhancement with workforce."

## Key Findings

### The Turning Point Thesis
- **2026 = pivot from pilots to enterprise scaling**
- 97% have begun or plan significant transformation initiatives
- 58% expect AI to be major growth engine within 2 years
- 32% believe AI will fundamentally reshape operations
- Only 20% say AI has significantly exceeded expectations (gap between promise and delivery)

### CEO Confidence Paradox
- **CEO Confidence Index:** 78.5 (down from 83.0 in Q3 2025)
- Yet **90% expect revenue growth and productivity gains** to support profitability
- Tension: pessimistic about macro, optimistic about own operations
- Translation: "We can't control the world, but we can control our AI/M&A strategy"

### Workforce Mindset Shift
- **46% → 24%** believing AI will reduce headcount (Jan 2025 → Dec 2025)
- **69%** now believe AI will maintain or expand employment
- Reframe from "automation = job cuts" to "augmentation = productivity"
- 79% optimistic about attracting/retaining critical talent

### CEO Priorities for 2026
- **40%** prioritize customer engagement and retention
- **37%** focus on product/process innovation
- **43%** emphasize operational optimization and AI/digitalization

### M&A & Strategic Alliances
- **53%** plan acquisitions aligned with growth priorities (+5% vs. Q3 2025)
- **79%** plan joint ventures/strategic alliances in 2026 (up from 62% in 2025)
- Shift toward flexibility: alliances > outright ownership

### Trust as Currency
- EY emphasizes trust is "becoming currency" in an era of deepfakes and AI-generated content
- Credibility differentiation emerging as competitive advantage
- Implicit positioning: transformation requires trusted advisors (like EY)

## Bottom Line

**Read it for:** The workforce mindset shift (automation → augmentation) is signal worth tracking. If CEOs stopped believing AI primarily cuts costs, that changes investment priorities and organizational design.

**Skip it for:** Technical AI guidance or implementation playbooks. This is strategic framing for CEOs, not tactical execution.

**Filter for:** EY's commercial interest in transformation complexity. The more complex the narrative (workforce transformation, not just automation), the more consulting hours EY sells. "Turning point" language creates urgency that benefits advisory practices.

**Key insight:** The gap between expectation and delivery is real—only 20% say AI exceeded expectations, yet 97% are investing in transformation. This suggests either: (a) most organizations are behind the curve and need to catch up, or (b) expectations were inflated and need calibration. EY argues (a) because it's sellable. Reality is probably both.

**Bias check:** EY benefits from both AI enthusiasm (transformation consulting) and AI complexity (workforce programs, governance, trust frameworks). The "turning point" framing is designed to move budget from pilot-stage experiments to enterprise-scale programs—which is where Big Four consulting revenue lives.

**Bottom line for strategists:** If 69% of CEOs now believe AI maintains/expands employment rather than reducing it, we're seeing a strategic reframe from "cost reduction" to "capability enhancement." This has implications for budgeting (OpEx vs. CapEx), org design (augmentation vs. replacement), and change management (upskilling vs. restructuring). Whether this reframe is based on evidence or wishful thinking remains unclear.
